Organizing Partners


UNESCO

United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization

The UNESCO office in Bangkok was established in 1961. UNESCO is an agency of the United Nations (UN) with the purpose to contribute to peace and security by promoting international collaboration through educational, scientific, and cultural reforms in order to increase universal respect for justice, the rule of law, and human rights along with fundamental freedom proclaimed in the United Nations Charter.


FOSSASIA

Open Tech Development in Asia

FOSSASIA develops Open Source software and hardware for conversational AI, science and event management with a global developer community from its base in Asia. FOSSASIA organizes Open Technology events, and runs coding programs. The annual FOSSASIA Summit is a premier Open Technology event in Asia. FOSSASIA was founded in 2009.

UNESCO TechCul is an online program to develop technology ideas for the creative and culture sector set over a period from November 2020 to January 2021.

The Corona pandemic has created major disruptions to arts and culture practitioners, agencies and enterprises. While cultural institutions close we need new innovative approaches to continue our cultural exchange and fullfill the responsibility of education. Many arts and culture organizations already moved services online. The increasingly widespread adoption of technological innovations in the sector demonstrate that they could lead to more sustainable business models, wider access to arts and culture products and services, better networking and exchanges, and above all, a more sustainable environment of the sector. But, we need to do more and we need to develop ideas. So, let's get started together!

Challenges of Culture and Creative Sector

Decling Revenue due to Covid-19

A decreasing number of visitors lead to unstable and low revenue: we are looking for innovative business models and new income streams that help sustain culture and art institutions including museums, galleries, monuments and heritage sites. Notes: Consider new ways to monetize for players in culture sector. Examples: paid virtual tours.

Accessibility

How can we use technology to make cultural products and resources available to people with limited access? Examples: For disadvantaged people (digital divide, weak Internet access, older people, disabled people etc.) beyond the middle class what solutions could we imagine and create?

Income Inequality

How can artists, creators and cultural institutions benefit from new revenue streams or get fairer remuneration from the audience or digital companies? Examples: Online sales sharing with platform providers. What are potentially models that allow artists to get a better share from their work? Or what are alternative business models to allow the consumption of artistic work?

Cultural Heritage Threats

Heritage’s increasing exposure to human-made and natural threats: How can we use technology to protect our heritage? Examples: threat reporting app, raise awareness, call for actions

Unsystematic data and knowledge management

How can we collect and analyze visitors data? How can we organize, process the existing data and resources of the culture and art sector and turn them to useful information and accessible knowledge. Examples: ticket/space booking application, crowd control, visitors feedback collection, object scanning app, self-guided app.

Lack of collaboration between stakeholders

How can digital platforms offer new possibilities for closer networking and collaboration among those working in culture and the arts. Examples: Those solutions that allow you to learn good practices from institutions and individuals in the same conditions, to help you access other steps of the value chain (producers, raw materials providers, technical specialists, etc.), to provide you with mentorship and advice to advance your work, etc.

Weakened practice and transmission

How can we safeguard and promote intangible cultural heritage? How can we encourage regular practice and learning within communities and between generations. Examples: online courses, gamification, digital books

Low public engagement

How can we increase public engagement in protection of heritage? Can communities help to project heritage? Examples: threat reporting app

Lack of capacity

Build capacity for cultural institutions, artists and cultural practitioners in the use of digital technologies to best benefit from the transformation toward the digital economy. Example: online courses, learning apps, etc.

A little more about UNESCO TechCul

UNESCO is launching TechCul to bring together tech and culture leaders with startups and entrepreneurs to develop win-win digital innovative technology solutions and business models to address the challenges faced in the culture and creative sector in times of a global pandemic.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the expected outcome of the ideathon?

The expected outcome of the ideathon are ideas that tackle challenges in the culture and creative sector. Please provide 1. A one-page idea brief (required) using our standard template, 2. A slidedeck presentation (required) outlining the details on challenges/problems, users, ideas, your team and possibilities of implementations and 3. An optional 2-3 minute video about your idea (optional).

When we talk about the culture and creative sector, what do we mean?

Culture and creative works can be considered parts of every aspect of live on a broader sense. When we think of the creative sector we usually refer to organizations or institutions particularly working in the area. Think of museums, science centres, UNESCO heritage sites, performaing arts such as theatre and music or cinegraphic arts and films.

What challenges should the ideathon address in the culture and creative sector?

There are many challenges the culture in creative sector is currently facing. Here are a few keywords from our list of challenges, that could get you started: Decling revenue due to Covid-19, income inequality, accessibility, lack of collaboration between stakeholders, unsystematic data and knowledge management, cultural heritage threats, weakened practice and transmission, low public engagement, lack of capacity:

Where can I find more info about the rules and submission guidelines?

The rules and submission guidelines are listed below in this page. Participants are required to register here and upload their work during the ideathon before the deadline. An upload link will be shared with all participants a few days before the ideathon event.




Can I submit a video to demonstrate our hack?

We love videos! The platform accepts links to YouTube, Vimeo or Youku. You can post a short video to demonstrate your work.

Is it possible to show other files?

There is also an option to upload a file. The platform allows submitters to upload one file, though they can combine files into a single ZIP file.

What other information do I need to provide?

The platform requires contestants to enter an entry name and description. Please also accept the the conditions of the contest including sharing your work under certified Open Source license.

What are the submission and display rights for our idea?

The submissions should be open. If any licensing is relevant, submissions should be submitted under an Open Source or Creative Commons By license. We also request the right to use the winners’ names and work to promote the competition and ideathons in future.

How does an ideathon work

At Ideathons participants develop ideas for projects that tackle a specific problem or area. Participants usually come from a variety of backgrounds and bring in unique perspectives. In UNESCO TechCul participants collaborate before the Ideathon event online on a platform and form teams. They also prepare for activities during the Ideathon event and research relevant topics. At the ideathon participants work together for the final submission which includes materials, presentations and videos about their idea. A few days after the online event the UNESCO selection team will announce the best ideas that will participate in a pitch event in January. An Ideathon is very similar to a Hackathon. In contrast to a Hackathon you focus more on the concept of a problem you would like to solve. It is a potential first step before developing an idea further into a technical solution or prototype. To give you a few impressions how an Ideathon works and how it can inspire people to develop prototypes check out this video which was shot at Science Hack Day San Francisco.
